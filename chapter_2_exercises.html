Solution to exercise 2.31

There are various approaches to compute the marginal distribution p(y) where y=x+z and x∼N(x∣μx​,Σx​), z∼N(z∣μz​,Σz​).

The first approach came to my mind after a video from 3Blue1Brown, that demonstrates exactly that in this case p(y)=p(x)∗p(z), where ∗ is the convolution operator.

p(y)=∫px​(y−t)pz​(t)dt

In this way, we consider every way to obtain y from the sum x+z. This solution has been adopted by Tommy Odland in his solutions.

But there is a simpler way to do this. Let's consider the conditional distribution p(y∣x). Since x is fixed, and y=x+z, the only variability is up to z. We can define this as

p(y∣x)=N(y∣μz​+x,Σz​)

We can now compare the obtained results with expressions 2.99 and 2.100:

μ=μx​Λ−1=Σx​A=Ib=μz​L−1=Σz​x=x

And using results from 2.109 and 2.110 we know:

E[y]=Aμ+b=Iμx​+μz​=μx​+μz​

and

cov[y]=L−1+AΛ−1AT=Σz​+Σx​
